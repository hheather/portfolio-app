The Camera Loves You

The camera loves you is an interactive installation depicting a CCTV camera that performs a display of affection towards its audience. By positioning viewers as the subject of this automated admiration, the piece interrogates society's growing dependence on artificial systems in ways that are becoming increasingly personalised and intimate. The work combines a Vision-Language model with a Large Language Model to generate personalised, affectionate responses directed at audience members.

It invites reflection on how such systems shape our feelings, influence our behaviour, and potentially erode our sense of agency.

Tools & Technologies

  • CRT
  • Computer Vision (QwenVL2.5)
  • LLM API (ChatGPT)
  • Python
  • Webcam

The Camera Loves You is a seeing, speaking CCTV camera that singles out members of its audience and addresses them directly, commenting on their appearance, calling them closer, performing a strange, one-sided intimacy. It draws on themes of surveillance, agency, and the automation of affection within AI systems.

  • Inspired by Mechanisms of Fascination by Rotor Studio, the piece uses a Vision-Language model (Qwen VL 2.5) to generate detailed descriptions of a randomly selected person in frame, their clothing, hair, pose, detected and tracked via YOLO.
  • Their proximity is estimated from their size relative to the frame, giving the system a sense of spatial awareness. These descriptions, along with positional data, are fed into an LLM prompted to address the person as though it's watching them, flattering, coaxing, asking them to step closer.
  • The output is displayed on-screen and spoken aloud via a custom text-to-speech program, displayed on a CRT monitor with the camera mounted overhead in a CCTV-style setup.

Learnings

This was my most technically ambitious project to date, combining vision-language models, LLMs, real-time person detection, and text-to-speech into a single interactive pipeline. My original concept was a speed-dating simulation where users would actively converse with an emotionally manipulative AI persona. I spent several weeks fine-tuning a local LLM on dialogue data inspired by the film Her, but found the outputs too unpredictable, highlighting just how much data is needed to reliably shape an LLM's personality.

The pivot to a passive, observational piece was a crucial design decision. User testing revealed that the conversational format felt like an interview, people had to perform for the AI rather than be affected by it. Removing that burden and letting the camera initiate the interaction made the experience far more unsettling and effective. It taught me that the most powerful interactions aren't always the most complex ones.

On the technical side, I learned a lot about evaluating and comparing VL models. Building the TTS component introduced its own challenges around threading and platform compatibility between macOS and Windows, and I made the deliberate choice to use built-in system voices rather than an API, both to reduce latency and to let the operating system's default voice become part of the piece's character. The CRT monitor and overhead CCTV-style camera positioning were late additions that significantly strengthened the conceptual framing around surveillance and artificial intimacy.
