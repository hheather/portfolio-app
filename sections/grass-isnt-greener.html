The Grass Isn't Greener

The Grass Isn't Greener is a satirical mini-game which explores the themes of reincarnation and environmental destruction. The player controls a ghost in heaven, aiming to increase the on-screen heart rate until the ghost is considered "alive", and is therefore reincarnated. However, upon achieving this supposed victory, the player is met with the bleak reality that Earth has been reduced to a barren desert. The game portrays a literal representation of the saying "the grass is always greener" as in this case, there is no grass at all.

Tools & Technologies

  • Unity
  • Arduino
  • Blender

The brief was to prototype an interactive experience with Unity and to design an external controller using Arduino with one simple interaction.

  • I was inspired by the ethereal-looking default sky box within Unity, which gave me the initial idea of using heaven as a basis for the concept.
  • The interaction is a third-person endless runner scenario, similar to Temple Run. This kept things simple, as the aim for me was to learn how to do location-based spawning based on the position of the player (cloud spawning).
  • For earth, I wanted to add the climate-disaster based twist at the end - for dramatic affect and silliness.
  • For the controller, I originally wanted to use a heart rate monitor, which I thought would make for an unusual and humorous interaction. However, due to issues with my monitor and time constraints, I opted for a simple stylised button in the shape of a heart. The player's speed was determined by how fast you pressed the button, communicated to Unity via Serial.

Learnings

This project was my first real experience working with Unity and Arduino together, and it taught me a lot about the practicalities of bridging physical and digital interactions. Setting up Serial communication between the two was more finicky than expected, and I learned the importance of keeping the interaction model simple when the technical pipeline is new. The location-based cloud spawning pushed me to think more carefully about procedural logic and performance, both spawning and cleaning up objects relative to the player's location. I also learned to adapt quickly when things don't go to plan; pivoting from the heart rate monitor to a physical button meant rethinking the interaction, but it ultimately resulted in something more intuitive and playful. If I were to revisit this, I'd love to explore biometric input properly and experiment with more dynamic environmental responses tied to the player's physical state.
